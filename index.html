Abstract

Orbital Skyhooks is the idea of a space transportation system which aims to reduce the cost and energy needed to launch payloads into orbit. Unlike a traditional rocket, which utilise chemical propulsion, a skyhook would use a rotating tether which would be situated in low Earth orbit to transfer momentum to a spacecraft, which pretty much means throwing them into higher orbits. This article will explore how an orbital skyhook could work, the physical principles behind momentum exchange, and why it would act as a stepping stone stone towards more advances space infrastructure such as a space elevator. The pros of skyhooks include reduced fuel consumption, cheaper launch costs and lessened environmental impact. However, there are some important challenges we will need to find solutions for, which include and not limited to, material strength, orbital stability and construction stability. Current research and small-scall experimental studies are talked about to assess whether skyhooks are feasible for the future and whether or not they would be practical.
